# Virtual Synesthesia — Research Findings

## Chromesthesia: Sound-to-Color Synesthesia

### Overview

Chromesthesia is a form of synesthesia in which **sound involuntarily evokes visual experiences** — colors, shapes, textures, and movement. It's one of the most common forms of synesthesia, affecting approximately 1 in 3,000 people for sound-color associations specifically, with ~4% of the population experiencing some form of synesthesia (Simner et al., 2006; Eagleman, 2009).

The experience is **automatic, consistent over time, and memorable** — a synesthete who hears middle C as red today will hear it as red in 10 years. This consistency is the diagnostic hallmark that distinguishes genuine synesthesia from metaphor or imagination.

### Key Papers

1. **Ramachandran & Hubbard (2001)** — "Synaesthesia: A window into perception, thought and language." *Journal of Consciousness Studies*, 8(12):3-34.
   - Proposed cross-activation theory: synesthetes have extra neural connections between adjacent brain regions due to a genetic mutation affecting neural pruning during development.

2. **Ward, Huckstep & Tsakanikos (2006)** — "Sound-colour synaesthesia: to what extent does it use cross-modal mechanisms common to us all?" *Cortex*, 42(2):264-280. **419 citations.**
   - Demonstrated that sound→color brightness (high pitch = bright, low pitch = dark) is universal — synesthetes and non-synesthetes both show the pattern. For synesthetes it's just conscious and involuntary.

3. **Hubbard & Ramachandran (2005)** — "Neurocognitive mechanisms of synesthesia." *Neuron*, 48(3):509-520.
   - fMRI evidence for cross-activation between V4 (color) and auditory regions in synesthetes.

4. **Itoh, Sakata, Kwee & Nakada (2017)** — "Musical pitch classes have rainbow hues in pitch class-color synesthesia." *Scientific Reports*.
   - Studied 15 pitch-class-color synesthetes. Found rainbow-like ordering: do=red through si=violet, with decreasing saturation. Enharmonic equivalents (C# vs Db) produce different colors — the color maps to the *name* of the note, not the absolute frequency. Supports a two-step model: pitch→note name→color.

5. **Itoh & Nakada (2018)** — "Absolute pitch is not necessary for pitch class-color synesthesia."
   - Confirmed rainbow pattern in 18 additional subjects without absolute pitch. Two-step process robustly confirmed.

6. **Cao & Ueda (2025)** — "One- or two-step? New insights into two-step hypothesis and rainbow-like theory for pitch class-color synesthesia."
   - Dual-task paradigm testing the robustness of the two-step hypothesis.

## Neurological Theories

### 1. Cross-Activation Theory (Ramachandran & Hubbard)
Extra structural connections between adjacent brain areas — specifically between auditory cortex and V4 (color area) in the fusiform gyrus — cause sound to directly trigger color perception. The genetic basis involves reduced neural pruning during early development.

### 2. Disinhibited Feedback Model (Cytowic & Eagleman)
All brains have feedback pathways between sensory areas, but synesthetes fail to inhibit these normally. This explains acquired synesthesia (after brain injury or sensory loss) and drug-induced synesthesia (psychedelics reduce inhibition). It's not extra wiring — it's failing to suppress existing wiring.

### 3. Hyperconnectivity Framework
DTI and fMRI studies show atypical white matter connectivity and functional coupling between sensory regions in synesthetes. Runs in families with a genetic component (40% of synesthetes have a first-degree relative with it), modulated by epigenetic factors.

### 4. Ideasthesia (Nikolić & Simner)
Synesthesia isn't pure sensory cross-wiring — it's mediated by *meaning*. The concept/idea of a stimulus (e.g., the note "C") triggers the concurrent (e.g., red), not the raw sensation of the frequency. This is why C# and Db (same frequency, different name) produce different colors in many synesthetes.

## Personal Accounts

### Classical Composers

**Alexander Scriabin (1872–1915)**
Developed a systematic mapping of keys to colors, ordered by the circle of fifths along the spectrum:
- C = Red, G = Orange-pink, D = Yellow, A = Green, E = Whitish-blue, F# = Bright blue, Db = Violet, Ab = Purplish-violet
- Debate exists whether Scriabin had genuine chromesthesia vs. learned/theosophical associations. His key-color system was published in 1911.

**Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov (1844–1908)**
Friend of Scriabin. Their sound-color associations were NOT the same. Rimsky-Korsakov distinguished major vs. minor keys and had "more neutral, spontaneous" associations. His mappings were different from Scriabin's despite them being contemporaries.

**Amy Beach (1867–1944)**
American composer who consistently associated keys with colors from childhood:
- C major = White, G major = Red, A major = Green, Ab major = Blue
- Minor keys = Black
- These associations influenced her compositional practice, especially nature/spiritual works.

**Franz Liszt (1811–1886)**
Gave instructions to orchestras in color terms: "Gentlemen, a little bluer, if you please" (1842 Weimar rehearsal). "That is a deep violet, please, depend on it!" He conceived of orchestral timbre in explicitly chromatic terms.

**Olivier Messiaen (1908–1992)**
Described experiencing "a sort of synesthesia" as an "inward reality." Constructed "color chords" based on his associations. Mode 2 (octatonic scale) = "blue-violet with gold and red." Works like *Chronochromie* (1960) and *Couleurs de la cité céleste* (1963) explicitly link musical form to color.

**Duke Ellington (1899–1974)**
Regarded his orchestra as a painter's palette. Trumpet = "dark blue." Alto saxophone = "light blue satin." Each performance was "creating a new painting."

**Leonard Bernstein (1918–1990)**
Described his chromesthesia as "timbre to color." Would stop orchestras and singers when the timbre changed because "the color changed."

### Contemporary Musicians

**Tori Amos**
> "The song appears as light filament once I've cracked it. As long as I've been doing this, which is more than thirty-five years, I've never seen the same light creature in my life. … Try to imagine the best kaleidoscope ever — after the initial excitement, you start to focus on each element's stunning original detail. … The sound of the words with the sound of the chord progression combined with the rhythm manifests itself in a unique expression of the architecture of color-and-light."

**Pharrell Williams**
Based his album *Seeing Sounds* (2008) on his synesthetic experience. Describes seeing music as colors and patterns that influence his production choices.

**Lorde**
Chromesthesia directly influences her songwriting. Arranges colors by theme and emotion within songs. Has spoken about it in multiple interviews.

**Billie Eilish**
Experiences synesthesia affecting music creation and visual aesthetics. Collaborates closely on visuals because she "sees" the songs.

Other confirmed synesthete musicians: **Kanye West, Aphex Twin, Billy Joel, Stevie Wonder, Dev Hynes (Blood Orange), Charli XCX, Frank Ocean, Mary J. Blige, Eddie Van Halen, Itzhak Perlman, Jean Sibelius.**

### Documented Synesthete (Non-Musician)

**Deni Simon** (via Cytowic)
> "Music produces waving lines — like oscilloscope configurations — lines moving in color, often metallic with height, width, and, most importantly, depth. My favorite music has lines that extend horizontally beyond the screen area."

## Specific Mapping Patterns

### Pitch → Brightness (Universal)
**The most robust finding across all research:**
- **High pitch → bright/light colors**
- **Low pitch → dark colors**
- This holds for synesthetes AND non-synesthetes (Ward et al., 2006)
- Synesthetes just experience it consciously and automatically

### Pitch Class → Hue (Rainbow Theory)
Itoh et al. (2017) found a rainbow-like progression across 33 subjects:
- **do (C) = Red** (warm, saturated)
- **re (D) = Yellow** (warm, bright)
- **mi–la (E–A) = Transitional** (yellow→green→blue range)
- **si (B) = Violet** (cool, desaturated)
- Higher pitch classes have **decreasing saturation**
- This maps to note NAMES, not absolute frequencies

### Timbre → Color & Texture
- **26% of music synesthetes** have timbre-specific color responses
- Different instruments playing the same note produce DIFFERENT colors
- Duke Ellington: trumpet = dark blue, alto sax = light blue satin
- Bernstein experienced "timbre to color" — different tone qualities = different hues
- Liszt: conceived orchestral timbre in chromatic terms

### Loudness Effects
- Loudness has notably **LESS influence** than pitch or timbre (Niccolai et al., 2012)
- Louder sounds → slightly **larger, brighter** photisms
- Cytowic: "Sound often changes the perceived hue, brightness, scintillation, and directional movement"

### Sound Parameter Summary

| Sound Property | Visual Effect |
|---------------|---------------|
| **Pitch** | Hue changes (rainbow progression); higher = brighter + less saturated |
| **Timbre** | Distinct colors per instrument; texture shifts; specific "tone color" |
| **Loudness** | Size + brightness increase (weaker effect than pitch/timbre) |
| **Rhythm/Tempo** | Speed of movement; pulsing; directional flow |
| **Harmony** | Color blending; chord = layered/mixed colors; dissonance = clashing/unpleasant colors |

## Shape, Texture & Spatial Characteristics

### Form Constants (Klüver)
Heinrich Klüver (1920s) identified 4 recurring form categories in hallucinations, also common in chromesthesia:
1. **Tunnels** (funnels, cones)
2. **Spirals**
3. **Honeycombs** (lattices, checkerboards)
4. **Gratings** (cobwebs)

### Descriptions from Synesthetes
- **Cytowic**: "Something like fireworks" — colors arise, move around, fade when sound ends
- **Deni Simon**: "Waving lines… like oscilloscope configurations… metallic, with height, width, and depth"
- **Tori Amos**: "Light filament" structures, "architecture of color-and-light," "kaleidoscope"
- **Kandinsky**: Sound translated into abstract forms — lines, circles, colored shapes in motion

### Spatial Dimensions
- **Projectors** experience colors in **external space** — as if projected onto a screen or the environment
- **Associators** experience colors in their **"mind's eye"** — internal, but still involuntary
- Colors typically appear in the **visual field** (not at the sound source)
- **Three-dimensional depth** is common — colors have position, depth, and movement
- Lines often "extend beyond the screen area" (Simon) — suggest infinite/unbounded canvas

---

## Temporal Dynamics

- Colors arise **with the sound onset**, persist while sound continues, and **fade when sound stops**
- 75% experience concurrents only while notes are actively playing
- Only ~33% can voluntarily control or suppress the experience
- Movement is **directional** — colors flow, oscillate, spiral in response to musical motion
- "Scintillation" — flicker/sparkle quality — changes with sound texture and attack
- Complex music → complex, layered visual fields with multiple simultaneous color streams

---

## Individual Differences vs. Commonalities

### What Varies Between Individuals
- **Specific note-color mappings** — no two synesthetes agree on exact colors for specific notes
- **Projector vs. Associator** — external projection vs. internal mind's eye
- **Triggers** — some respond to all sounds, others only to music, others only to speech
- **Shape preferences** — some see geometric forms, others see organic/flowing shapes, others see oscilloscope lines
- **Color palette** — pastels vs. neons vs. earth tones

### What Is Consistent Across Synesthetes
- **Pitch→brightness correlation** (high=bright, low=dark) — universal
- **Rainbow ordering of pitch classes** — do=warm, si=cool — reliable pattern
- **Involuntary, automatic nature** — can't be turned off
- **Consistency over time** — test-retest reliability is near-perfect
- **Emotional component** — the experience is typically described as beautiful, euphoric, or deeply meaningful
- **Developmental onset** — appears in early childhood, typically before age 7

## Savickaite et al. (2023) — VR Recreation of Synesthetic Experience

**"Using immersive virtual reality to recreate the synaesthetic experience"** — Savickaite, S., McNaughton, D., Gaigalas, R., & Ward, J. (2023). *i-Perception*, 14(3). [PMC10478570](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10478570/)

### Overview
Two experiments recreating synesthetic experiences in VR (OpenBrush). Six synesthetes in Experiment 1 ("VR-by-proxy"), three audiovisual synesthetes in Experiment 2 (self-drawing in VR). Key finding: VR dramatically outperforms 2D color pickers for capturing the richness of auditory-visual synesthesia — movement, texture, 3D space, and animation are essential.

### Why VR/3D matters
- **P1**: "This is the best I've ever gotten to be honest. This is the best I've ever seen that can describe the sort of stars in your eyes… I never got to represent my experiences so clearly."
- **P2**: "There's so much more that I can convey and describe about it because I'm able to use like a 3D space rather than trying to draw something on a piece of paper. So yeah, a massive advantage."
- **P3**: "You need to just draw what I can see. Pen and paper are too small and doesn't get the same accurate representation."

### Specific Visual Descriptions (used in Virtual Synesthesia visualizers)

**Texture & Material Qualities:**
- "This smoke will actually come in really helpful because a lot of my synaesthesia looks kind of like that" (P1)
- "The embers would still be going over the top as well. You can see the embers through it" (P1)
- "a little bit fuzzy" (P1)
- "smokiness" and "cloudiness" of sensations (P2)
- "stony, rough" textures (P2)
- "as if it had been cut by a serrated knife" (P3)
- "yellow and sparkly" (P1 — birdsong)
- "electric bits" (P1 — guitar sounds)

**Movement Patterns:**
- "in the middle it's slower, but on the outside it moves a lot more, almost like a sort of gravitational vacuum" (P1)
- "pulsating outwards as if it's churning round" (P1)
- "floating away from the shape… starts strong and it gets quieter" (P2)
- "circular motion" was "quite important" (P1)
- Directional movement, swaying, "solid" slow movement
- Smoke-like, serrated/wave-like movements

**Shape & Spatial Characteristics:**
- "big yellow shape and then like a sort of orange line in the middle" (P1)
- "sideways eye shape in the middle" (P2)
- "the general like hard shape" (P2)
- Three-dimensional shapes specific to the instrument (P4)
- Shapes moving up or down in space depending on pitch (P4)
- Colors and shapes had "specific placements in space" (P3)

**Color Patterns:**
- Different shades of a color within the same experience (e.g., different shades of blue for electric bits)
- Silver/metallic for guitar scratches
- Blue tones with animated directional movement for cars passing
- Specific colors tied to specific sounds — consistent within individuals

### How This Informs Virtual Synesthesia Design
1. **Persistence**: Synesthetic experiences "persist while sound continues and fade when sound stops" — our canvas uses alpha=0.05 clearing for rich trails
2. **Texture over geometry**: Smoke, embers, cloudiness, fuzz — not clean geometric lines
3. **Movement is essential**: Pulsating, floating, churning, gravitational pull — all layers animate continuously
4. **Unified 3D-like space**: Elements exist in a shared depth field, not as separate overlays
5. **Sensitivity to quiet**: Even subtle sounds produce "sparkly," "electric," "shimmering" qualities — stardust spawns at very low volumes
